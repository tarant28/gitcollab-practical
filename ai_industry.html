Artificial Intelligence (AI)

Healthcare

Artificial intelligence is transforming healthcare by enabling earlier and more accurate disease detection, personalising treatment plans based on individual patient data, and accelerating drug discovery to bring life-saving therapies to market faster. AI-powered tools also support virtual health assistants and streamline administrative work, freeing clinicians to focus on the human side of care. In short, AI is helping improve outcomes, increase access to care and reduce costs across the health system.

Key applications

  • Medical imaging: computer vision helps detect anomalies in X-rays, CT and MRI, assisting radiologists with triage and second reads.
  • Predictive analytics: models identify patients at risk of readmission, sepsis, or deterioration to enable earlier intervention.
  • Personalised medicine: algorithms match therapies to genetic markers and patient profiles and support dosing optimisation.
  • Clinical decision support: summarise patient histories and surface guideline-based recommendations during consultations.
  • Operational efficiency: automate coding, prior authorisations, scheduling, and claims review to reduce admin burden.
  • Virtual care: chatbots and ambient scribe tools support remote monitoring and documentation.

Benefits and challenges

Benefits Challenges
Faster diagnoses Data quality and bias
Fewer errors Privacy and security
More time for patient care Model explainability
Lower costs Clinical validation
Broader access via telehealth Regulatory approval

Real-world examples

  • Radiology triage flags suspected strokes and fractures for priority review.
  • Early-warning systems monitor vitals to predict sepsis risk hours before onset.
  • Drug discovery platforms explore molecule candidates and propose targets to shorten R&D cycles.

Environment and Sustainability

AI contributes to environmental sustainability by helping scientists model climate patterns more precisely, optimising energy consumption in buildings and smart grids, and monitoring wildlife or illegal activities to protect ecosystems. By embedding intelligence into our systems, we are better equipped to reduce carbon footprints, recycle resources and safeguard the planet for future generations.

Where AI helps

  • Energy: forecast demand, optimise grid dispatch, integrate renewables, and detect faults in wind/solar assets.
  • Buildings: smart controls learn occupancy patterns to tune HVAC and lighting, cutting energy use.
  • Agriculture: precision farming analyses soil, weather, and satellite data to reduce water and fertiliser.
  • Climate science: downscaling models and emulators speed up simulations and uncertainty analysis.
  • Monitoring: computer vision from drones/satellites tracks deforestation, wildfires, and illegal fishing.
  • Waste and recycling: vision systems sort materials and optimise collection routes.

Impact and considerations

  • Impact: lower emissions, improved resilience, and better measurement of progress.
  • Considerations: data gaps in the Global South, embodied energy of compute, and the need for transparent, verifiable metrics.
  • Best practice: pair AI with domain expertise, open data where possible, and strong governance to avoid greenwashing.

Business and Economy

AI is rapidly reshaping business and the economy by automating routine tasks, enabling smarter fraud detection in finance, powering personalised customer experiences and creating entirely new industries. In effect, AI is becoming a core driver of productivity growth, innovation and economic transformation across sectors.

Use cases across functions

  • Marketing and sales: customer segmentation, propensity modelling, next-best action, and dynamic pricing.
  • Operations: demand forecasting, inventory optimisation, route planning, and predictive maintenance.
  • Finance and risk: anomaly detection for fraud, credit scoring, and automated reconciliation.
  • Customer service: chatbots, summarisation, and agent assist to reduce handle time and improve satisfaction.
  • HR and talent: candidate screening, skills mapping, and internal mobility matching.
  • Product: recommendation systems and generative design to accelerate experimentation.

Productivity and workforce

  • Task automation shifts roles toward higher-value judgement and creativity; workflows are redesigned rather than removed wholesale.
  • Augmented tools raise output for both novices and experts; training and change management determine realised gains.
  • Reskilling in data literacy, prompt engineering, and governance becomes a core investment.

Governance and risk

  • Establish model risk management: testing, monitoring, and rollback plans.
  • Address privacy, IP, and bias with policies, human oversight, and audit trails.
  • Measure ROI with pilot-to-production playbooks and clear KPIs aligned to business goals.
