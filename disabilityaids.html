Screen Readers

One of the most common disability aid tools in computing are screen readers, as they allow blind people to interact with computers without being dependant on a seeing person to assist them.

Screen reader software today often comes embedded into the operating system, eg, Windows Narrator, or Android's TalkBack. It converts sections of text on the screen into speech, and is often coupled with speech to text software so that the user can also talk back to the device - which is especially useful on devices like mobile phones, which do not provide tactile feedback when typing unlike a traditional keyboard.

Braille Displays

As screen readers read chunks of the screen at a time, they can often be paired with braille displays. Braille displays are most often a USB accessory that works with screen reader software to output text on the screen in a way blind people can digest, however instead of using speech synthesis, it uses a board with pins that can move up and down to form braille characters.

Dictation Software

Also mentioned on earlier, dictation software is used to input text into computers when you are unable to use a keyboard. Couple with screen readers they are very useful tool for blind people as they enable typing on mobile phones or other touchscreen devices which lack a physical keyboard. They are however also very useful for people with mobility or fine motor control issues, who would be unable to or struggle to use a keyboard. Word dictation software today is very reliable, with the New York Times saying that the dictation feature in Microsoft Word has 99% accuracy as of August 28th, 2025.
